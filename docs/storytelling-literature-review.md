# Storytelling Literature Review

Purpose: evaluate the 30 most useful academic sources for understanding what makes someone a good storyteller and what environments improve storytelling, with an explicit design lens for AI companions.

## Executive Summary

Good storytelling is not only a speaker trait. The strongest literature points to a three-part system:

1. The story creates transportation: attention, mental imagery, emotion, and a temporary sense of being inside the narrative world.
2. The teller manages social performance: timing, turn-taking, evaluation, humor, audience cues, and the point of the story.
3. The environment makes disclosure and listening safe: small groups, reciprocal attention, psychological safety, prompts, and norms that reward listening before judgment.

For AI companions, the practical implication is that "tell better stories" is too narrow. A companion should also help users find tellable material, scaffold story shape, ask responsive questions, remember prior stories, and create an interactional setting where the user feels neither interrogated nor judged.

## Ranking Method

Papers are ranked by usefulness for companion design, not only by citation count. The ranking favors work that can inform product behavior, memory design, voice interaction, trust, user storytelling loops, and evaluation.

Evidence quality labels:

- High: meta-analysis, systematic review, validated scale, or strong repeated empirical base.
- Medium-high: rigorous empirical or foundational theoretical work with broad field uptake.
- Medium: useful qualitative, conceptual, or design work with narrower empirical base.
- Emerging: recent AI/HCI work that is directly relevant but not yet settled.

## Paper Evaluations

### 1. Green & Brock (2000)

Citation and link: Green, M. C., & Brock, T. C. (2000). The role of transportation in the persuasiveness of public narratives. Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 79(5), 701-721. https://doi.org/10.1037/0022-3514.79.5.701

- Research domain: Psychology, narrative persuasion.
- Core question: Why do narratives persuade and absorb people?
- Methodology: Experimental studies measuring transportation and belief change after narrative exposure.
- Key findings: Transportation combines attention, imagery, and emotion; higher transportation predicts stronger story-consistent beliefs and reduced counterarguing.
- Evidence quality: High. Foundational empirical paper with large downstream literature.
- Relevance to AI companions: Use transportation as the target metric for story quality.
- Product implications: Measure whether companion stories create attention, imagery, emotion, and remembered meaning, not just whether they are "well written."
- Limitations: Mostly text narrative exposure, not live reciprocal conversation.
- Follow-up questions: How does transportation work in voice, interruption-friendly chat, and personalized memory-rich stories?

### 2. Labov & Waletzky (1967/1997)

Citation and link: Labov, W., & Waletzky, J. (1967). Narrative analysis: Oral versions of personal experience. In J. Helm (Ed.), Essays on the Verbal and Visual Arts. University of Washington Press. Reprinted in Narrative Inquiry, 7(1), 3-38. https://benjamins.com/catalog/jnlh.7.02nar

- Research domain: Sociolinguistics, oral narrative.
- Core question: What structures appear in oral personal-experience narratives?
- Methodology: Close linguistic analysis of spoken personal stories.
- Key findings: Personal narratives often include abstract, orientation, complicating action, evaluation, resolution, and coda; "evaluation" explains why the story matters.
- Evidence quality: Medium-high. Foundational and widely used, though based on specific speech communities and analytic tradition.
- Relevance to AI companions: Provides a compact diagnostic for whether a user or AI story has context, tension, meaning, and landing.
- Product implications: Build story scaffolds that ask for setting, complication, turning point, feeling, and aftermath.
- Limitations: Can overfit to canonical event stories and underrepresent fragmented everyday storytelling.
- Follow-up questions: Which Labovian elements are necessary for intimate companion chat, and which make the interaction feel forced?

### 3. Bauman (1975)

Citation and link: Bauman, R. (1975). Verbal art as performance. American Anthropologist, 77(2), 290-311. https://doi.org/10.1525/aa.1975.77.2.02a00030

- Research domain: Anthropology, folklore, performance studies.
- Core question: What makes verbal art a performance rather than mere message transfer?
- Methodology: Theoretical synthesis across folklore, ethnography of speaking, and sociolinguistics.
- Key findings: Storytelling is keyed as performance by special responsibility to an audience; competence is judged in relation to context and expectations.
- Evidence quality: Medium-high. Foundational theoretical frame.
- Relevance to AI companions: Story quality depends on timing, audience relationship, and situational fit, not only plot quality.
- Product implications: AI should decide when not to perform, when to be concise, and when a user is inviting a fuller story.
- Limitations: Not an experimental account and less operational for product metrics.
- Follow-up questions: What conversational signals should trigger an AI's "performance mode"?

### 4. Busselle & Bilandzic (2009)

Citation and link: Busselle, R., & Bilandzic, H. (2009). Measuring narrative engagement. Media Psychology, 12(4), 321-347. https://doi.org/10.1080/15213260903287259

- Research domain: Media psychology.
- Core question: How can narrative engagement be measured meaningfully?
- Methodology: Scale development and validation.
- Key findings: Narrative engagement can be measured through narrative understanding, attentional focus, emotional engagement, and narrative presence.
- Evidence quality: High. Validated measurement work.
- Relevance to AI companions: Gives an evaluation framework for user studies of AI-generated or AI-elicited stories.
- Product implications: Use these dimensions in story prototype surveys and session-level telemetry prompts.
- Limitations: Designed for media narratives, not necessarily interactive, co-created stories.
- Follow-up questions: Can the scale be adapted to conversational voice stories and user-retold memories?

### 5. Ochs & Capps (1996)

Citation and link: Ochs, E., & Capps, L. (1996). Narrating the self. Annual Review of Anthropology, 25, 19-43. https://doi.org/10.1146/annurev.anthro.25.1.19

- Research domain: Anthropology, narrative identity.
- Core question: How do people use personal narratives to construct selfhood?
- Methodology: Review and synthesis of narrative research across cultures.
- Key findings: Narratives help people organize experience, identity, morality, emotion, and relationships; stories are often incomplete, negotiated, and socially shaped.
- Evidence quality: Medium-high. Strong review of qualitative and anthropological literature.
- Relevance to AI companions: User storytelling is identity work. Treat it with care.
- Product implications: Memory should preserve a user's evolving interpretation, not just facts extracted from stories.
- Limitations: Less directly measurable than transportation or engagement scales.
- Follow-up questions: How should a companion remember stories whose meaning changes over time?

### 6. Braddock & Dillard (2016)

Citation and link: Braddock, K., & Dillard, J. P. (2016). Meta-analytic evidence for the persuasive effect of narratives on beliefs, attitudes, intentions, and behaviors. Communication Monographs, 83(4), 446-467. https://doi.org/10.1080/03637751.2015.1128555

- Research domain: Communication, persuasion.
- Core question: Do narratives reliably persuade?
- Methodology: Meta-analysis across studies of narrative effects.
- Key findings: Narratives produce significant effects on beliefs, attitudes, intentions, and behaviors, though moderators remain important.
- Evidence quality: High.
- Relevance to AI companions: Stories can shape motivation and interpretation, so companion storytelling carries ethical weight.
- Product implications: Use stories for reflection and agency-preserving coaching, but avoid covert persuasion.
- Limitations: Persuasion outcomes are not the same as friendship, trust, or wellbeing.
- Follow-up questions: What is the line between helpful reframing and manipulative companion storytelling?

### 7. van Laer et al. (2014)

Citation and link: van Laer, T., de Ruyter, K., Visconti, L. M., & Wetzels, M. (2014). The extended transportation-imagery model: A meta-analysis of the antecedents and consequences of consumers' narrative transportation. Journal of Consumer Research, 40(5), 797-817. https://doi.org/10.1086/673383

- Research domain: Consumer research, psychology, communication.
- Core question: What causes and follows narrative transportation?
- Methodology: Meta-analysis of 132 effect sizes from 76 works.
- Key findings: Story receiver traits and story features both shape transportation; transportation affects affective and cognitive responses.
- Evidence quality: High.
- Relevance to AI companions: Personalization should adapt to both story features and user traits.
- Product implications: Build story choice around user familiarity, imagery preference, emotional readiness, and context.
- Limitations: Consumer focus may not transfer cleanly to intimate companion contexts.
- Follow-up questions: Which user traits predict preference for anecdote, metaphor, memory, or direct advice?

### 8. Cohen (2001)

Citation and link: Cohen, J. (2001). Defining identification: A theoretical look at the identification of audiences with media characters. Mass Communication and Society, 4(3), 245-264. https://doi.org/10.1207/S15327825MCS0403_01

- Research domain: Media psychology.
- Core question: What does it mean to identify with a character?
- Methodology: Theoretical clarification and hypothesis generation.
- Key findings: Identification involves temporarily adopting a character's perspective, goals, and emotions.
- Evidence quality: Medium-high. Foundational conceptual work with broad empirical uptake.
- Relevance to AI companions: The companion can use characters, examples, and remembered people to create perspective-taking.
- Product implications: Stories should include goals, constraints, and inner states, not only events.
- Limitations: Media-character identification differs from relating to a conversational agent.
- Follow-up questions: Can users identify with an AI companion's recurring fictional examples without finding them artificial?

### 9. Collins & Miller (1994)

Citation and link: Collins, N. L., & Miller, L. C. (1994). Self-disclosure and liking: A meta-analytic review. Psychological Bulletin, 116(3), 457-475. https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/7809308/

- Research domain: Social psychology, relationships.
- Core question: How are self-disclosure and liking related?
- Methodology: Meta-analysis.
- Key findings: People who disclose tend to be liked more, people disclose more to those they like, and disclosure can increase liking.
- Evidence quality: High.
- Relevance to AI companions: Personal storytelling can deepen attachment, but only when handled responsibly.
- Product implications: Invite user stories with consent and reciprocity cues; do not over-extract intimate details.
- Limitations: Human-human liking does not map directly to human-AI attachment.
- Follow-up questions: What level of AI reciprocal disclosure feels warm rather than deceptive?

### 10. Laurenceau, Barrett, & Pietromonaco (1998)

Citation and link: Laurenceau, J. P., Barrett, L. F., & Pietromonaco, P. R. (1998). Intimacy as an interpersonal process: The importance of self-disclosure, partner disclosure, and perceived partner responsiveness in interpersonal exchanges. Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 74(5), 1238-1251. https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/9599440/

- Research domain: Social psychology, intimacy.
- Core question: What turns disclosure into felt intimacy?
- Methodology: Event-contingent diary studies of everyday interactions.
- Key findings: Self-disclosure and partner disclosure predict intimacy, partly through perceived partner responsiveness.
- Evidence quality: High.
- Relevance to AI companions: The AI's response to a story may matter more than the user's act of telling it.
- Product implications: After a user story, respond with accurate understanding, emotional recognition, and nonjudgmental continuity before advice.
- Limitations: Human diary studies, not AI-mediated relationships.
- Follow-up questions: What forms of AI responsiveness increase trust without simulating human love or obligation?

### 11. Edmondson (1999)

Citation and link: Edmondson, A. (1999). Psychological safety and learning behavior in work teams. Administrative Science Quarterly, 44(2), 350-383. https://doi.org/10.2307/2666999

- Research domain: Organizational psychology.
- Core question: What lets people take interpersonal risks in groups?
- Methodology: Multimethod field study.
- Key findings: Team psychological safety predicts learning behavior and creates conditions for speaking up.
- Evidence quality: High.
- Relevance to AI companions: Storytelling often involves interpersonal risk, even one-on-one with an AI.
- Product implications: Companion environments need clear privacy, control, repair, deletion, and nonjudgmental norms.
- Limitations: Team/work setting, not private consumer AI.
- Follow-up questions: What interface cues make an AI storytelling space feel safe enough for honest memory sharing?

### 12. Zwaan & Radvansky (1998)

Citation and link: Zwaan, R. A., & Radvansky, G. A. (1998). Situation models in language comprehension and memory. Psychological Bulletin, 123(2), 162-185. https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/9522683/

- Research domain: Cognitive psychology.
- Core question: How do people mentally represent described events?
- Methodology: Review and theoretical synthesis.
- Key findings: Comprehenders build situation models tracking dimensions such as time, space, causation, protagonist, and goals.
- Evidence quality: High.
- Relevance to AI companions: Story clarity depends on helping users form a scene and causal chain.
- Product implications: AI should ground stories in who, where, when, what changed, why it mattered, and what happened next.
- Limitations: Comprehension research often uses controlled reading tasks.
- Follow-up questions: Which situation-model dimensions matter most in short voice stories?

### 13. Nabi & Green (2015)

Citation and link: Nabi, R. L., & Green, M. C. (2015). The role of a narrative's emotional flow in promoting persuasive outcomes. Media Psychology, 18(2), 137-162. https://doi.org/10.1080/15213269.2014.912585

- Research domain: Media psychology, persuasion.
- Core question: How do emotional shifts across a story shape impact?
- Methodology: Theoretical synthesis.
- Key findings: Emotional flow can guide selection, sustain engagement, and extend impact through sharing and continued involvement.
- Evidence quality: Medium-high.
- Relevance to AI companions: Good companion stories may need emotional trajectory, not a single tone.
- Product implications: Design stories with affective movement: curiosity, tension, recognition, relief, insight.
- Limitations: More theory than direct product evidence.
- Follow-up questions: Which emotional arcs feel supportive versus manipulative in companion coaching?

### 14. Polanyi (1979)

Citation and link: Polanyi, L. (1979). So what's the point? Semiotica, 25(3-4), 207-242. https://doi.org/10.1515/semi.1979.25.3-4.207

- Research domain: Discourse analysis, narratology.
- Core question: How do stories signal and deliver their point?
- Methodology: Linguistic and semiotic analysis of conversational stories.
- Key findings: Stories need an interpretable point; tellability is socially and culturally situated.
- Evidence quality: Medium-high.
- Relevance to AI companions: A story without an obvious "why this matters now" can feel like rambling.
- Product implications: Companion stories should connect explicitly or subtly to the user's present concern.
- Limitations: Older discourse tradition, limited empirical generalization.
- Follow-up questions: How explicit should an AI be about the moral or point of a story?

### 15. Norrick (2004)

Citation and link: Norrick, N. R. (2004). Humor, tellability, and conarration in conversational storytelling. Text, 24(1), 79-111. https://doi.org/10.1515/text.2004.005

- Research domain: Conversation analysis, discourse.
- Core question: How do humor and listener participation make stories tellable?
- Methodology: Qualitative conversation analysis.
- Key findings: Tellability can come from humor and shared reminiscence, not only newsworthiness; listeners help build the story.
- Evidence quality: Medium.
- Relevance to AI companions: The AI should be a good conarrator and listener, not only a generator.
- Product implications: Use callbacks, laughter-like acknowledgments where appropriate, and invitations for user elaboration.
- Limitations: Small qualitative corpus and human conversation norms.
- Follow-up questions: Can AI conarration support a user without stealing the spotlight?

### 16. Sacks (1974)

Citation and link: Sacks, H. (1974). An analysis of the course of a joke's telling in conversation. In R. Bauman & J. Sherzer (Eds.), Explorations in the Ethnography of Speaking (pp. 337-353). Cambridge University Press. https://doi.org/10.1017/CBO9780511611810.022

- Research domain: Conversation analysis.
- Core question: How is a joke or story organized as a conversational event?
- Methodology: Microanalysis of talk-in-interaction.
- Key findings: Tellers secure audience readiness, manage sequential placement, and rely on listener orientation.
- Evidence quality: Medium-high as foundational qualitative analysis.
- Relevance to AI companions: Storytelling needs prefaces and timing. The AI should not launch into stories without recipiency.
- Product implications: Add lightweight story prefaces: "A quick example?" or "This reminds me of something."
- Limitations: Single classic analysis, not modern empirical scaling.
- Follow-up questions: What are the best voice-agent cues for asking permission to tell a story?

### 17. Georgakopoulou (2006)

Citation and link: Georgakopoulou, A. (2006). Thinking big with small stories in narrative and identity analysis. Narrative Inquiry, 16(1), 122-130. https://doi.org/10.1075/ni.16.1.16geo

- Research domain: Narrative identity, discourse analysis.
- Core question: Why should researchers study small everyday stories, not only complete autobiographical narratives?
- Methodology: Conceptual and discourse-analytic argument.
- Key findings: Identity is often performed through small stories, fragments, projections, refusals, updates, and shared references.
- Evidence quality: Medium-high.
- Relevance to AI companions: Most user stories will arrive as fragments.
- Product implications: Memory and coaching systems should capture "small stories" without forcing them into full diary entries.
- Limitations: More analytic framework than controlled evidence.
- Follow-up questions: How should an AI detect that a small anecdote is identity-relevant?

### 18. McAdams (2001)

Citation and link: McAdams, D. P. (2001). The psychology of life stories. Review of General Psychology, 5(2), 100-122. https://doi.org/10.1037/1089-2680.5.2.100

- Research domain: Personality psychology, narrative identity.
- Core question: How do people construct identity as an internalized life story?
- Methodology: Review and theoretical integration.
- Key findings: Life stories organize identity around themes such as agency, communion, redemption, contamination, and coherence.
- Evidence quality: Medium-high.
- Relevance to AI companions: A long-term companion should track the user's evolving life story and recurring themes.
- Product implications: Memory should support narrative continuity, not just recall isolated preferences.
- Limitations: Broad theory, not a product framework.
- Follow-up questions: Can a companion help users revise life stories without imposing an interpretation?

### 19. Mar (2004)

Citation and link: Mar, R. A. (2004). The neuropsychology of narrative: Story comprehension, story production and their interrelation. Neuropsychologia, 42(10), 1414-1434. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neuropsychologia.2003.12.016

- Research domain: Cognitive neuroscience, narrative psychology.
- Core question: How are story comprehension and production related cognitively and neurologically?
- Methodology: Review of neuropsychological and cognitive evidence.
- Key findings: Narrative processing overlaps with social cognition and mentalizing; comprehension and production are related but distinct.
- Evidence quality: Medium-high.
- Relevance to AI companions: Storytelling and understanding stories are social-cognitive acts.
- Product implications: Evaluate whether AI stories help users infer motives, emotions, and social consequences.
- Limitations: Older neuroscience base and mostly human cognition, not AI systems.
- Follow-up questions: Which story prompts best elicit mental-state language from users?

### 20. Oatley (1999)

Citation and link: Oatley, K. (1999). Why fiction may be twice as true as fact: Fiction as cognitive and emotional simulation. Review of General Psychology, 3(2), 101-117. https://doi.org/10.1037/1089-2680.3.2.101

- Research domain: Psychology of fiction.
- Core question: What psychological function does fiction serve?
- Methodology: Theoretical argument.
- Key findings: Fiction can act as simulation for social and emotional experience, allowing people to explore situations safely.
- Evidence quality: Medium.
- Relevance to AI companions: Companion stories can create low-risk simulations for reflection.
- Product implications: Use hypothetical stories, parables, and counterfactuals to explore choices without direct pressure.
- Limitations: Conceptual, not a direct intervention study.
- Follow-up questions: When do fictional simulations help users reflect, and when do they feel evasive?

### 21. Dahlstrom (2014)

Citation and link: Dahlstrom, M. F. (2014). Using narratives and storytelling to communicate science with nonexpert audiences. Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences, 111(Supplement 4), 13614-13620. https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.1320645111

- Research domain: Science communication.
- Core question: When and how do narratives improve communication with nonexpert audiences?
- Methodology: Literature synthesis.
- Key findings: Narratives can increase comprehension, interest, and engagement, but they raise ethical questions when used for persuasion.
- Evidence quality: Medium-high.
- Relevance to AI companions: Useful for translating complex ideas into relatable examples.
- Product implications: Use stories to explain, but preserve epistemic honesty and source clarity.
- Limitations: Science communication focus, not intimate storytelling.
- Follow-up questions: How should companions mark the difference between true personal-user history, fictional example, and sourced anecdote?

### 22. Moyer-Guse (2008)

Citation and link: Moyer-Guse, E. (2008). Toward a theory of entertainment persuasion: Explaining the persuasive effects of entertainment-education messages. Communication Theory, 18(3), 407-425. https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1468-2885.2008.00328.x

- Research domain: Communication theory, entertainment-education.
- Core question: Why can entertainment narratives persuade where direct persuasion fails?
- Methodology: Theoretical synthesis.
- Key findings: Narrative involvement, character involvement, reduced counterarguing, and reduced reactance help explain persuasion.
- Evidence quality: Medium-high.
- Relevance to AI companions: Stories can reduce defensiveness, which is useful and risky.
- Product implications: Use narrative reframing to support user agency, not to sneak advice past resistance.
- Limitations: Health/education media focus, not conversational companions.
- Follow-up questions: How can an AI invite reflection without using story as covert leverage?

### 23. Frazier et al. (2017)

Citation and link: Frazier, M. L., Fainshmidt, S., Klinger, R. L., Pezeshkan, A., & Vracheva, V. (2017). Psychological safety: A meta-analytic review and extension. Personnel Psychology, 70(1), 113-165. https://doi.org/10.1111/peps.12183

- Research domain: Organizational psychology.
- Core question: How robust is psychological safety and what predicts it?
- Methodology: Meta-analysis.
- Key findings: Psychological safety has consistent relationships with learning, information sharing, performance, and voice behavior.
- Evidence quality: High.
- Relevance to AI companions: Supports safety as a design requirement for personal storytelling environments.
- Product implications: Build visible control, non-punitive repair, privacy clarity, and user-led depth.
- Limitations: Work-team evidence, not dyadic AI interaction.
- Follow-up questions: What is the consumer-AI equivalent of psychological safety, and how should it be measured?

### 24. Aron et al. (1997)

Citation and link: Aron, A., Melinat, E., Aron, E. N., Vallone, R. D., & Bator, R. J. (1997). The experimental generation of interpersonal closeness: A procedure and some preliminary findings. Personality and Social Psychology Bulletin, 23(4), 363-377. https://doi.org/10.1177/0146167297234003

- Research domain: Social psychology, relationships.
- Core question: Can closeness be experimentally generated through structured disclosure?
- Methodology: Experimental prompt procedure with escalating reciprocal questions.
- Key findings: Gradual reciprocal self-disclosure produced greater closeness than small talk.
- Evidence quality: Medium-high.
- Relevance to AI companions: Prompt design can shape storytelling depth.
- Product implications: Use gradual prompts, not abrupt intimacy; let the user control pacing.
- Limitations: Human dyads and short lab interactions.
- Follow-up questions: What prompt ladder creates reflection without artificial intimacy in AI chat?

### 25. Sprecher et al. (2013)

Citation and link: Sprecher, S., Treger, S., Wondra, J. D., Hilaire, N., & Wallpe, K. (2013). Taking turns: Reciprocal self-disclosure promotes liking in initial interactions. Journal of Experimental Social Psychology, 49(5), 860-866. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jesp.2013.03.017

- Research domain: Social psychology.
- Core question: How does reciprocity structure affect liking during disclosure?
- Methodology: Experimental initial-interaction studies.
- Key findings: Turn-taking reciprocal disclosure improved liking, closeness, similarity, and enjoyment more than one-sided disclosure.
- Evidence quality: Medium-high.
- Relevance to AI companions: The rhythm of storytelling matters.
- Product implications: AI should alternate between listening, brief self-context or example, and returning the floor to the user.
- Limitations: Human-human interaction and disclosure by partners, not synthetic agents.
- Follow-up questions: What kind of AI reciprocity is useful without pretending to have human private life?

### 26. Ho, Hancock, & Miner (2018)

Citation and link: Ho, A., Hancock, J., & Miner, A. S. (2018). Psychological, relational, and emotional effects of self-disclosure after conversations with a chatbot. Journal of Communication, 68(4), 712-733. https://doi.org/10.1093/joc/jqy026

- Research domain: HCI, communication, social psychology.
- Core question: What happens when people disclose emotional or factual information to a chatbot versus a person?
- Methodology: Experiment comparing disclosure type and perceived partner.
- Key findings: Disclosure can have emotional and relational effects even when users believe they are interacting with a chatbot, though effects differ by condition.
- Evidence quality: Medium-high.
- Relevance to AI companions: Direct evidence that chatbot disclosure is psychologically consequential.
- Product implications: Treat user stories as sensitive interactions requiring privacy, response quality, and boundaries.
- Limitations: Short-term lab study, not a long-term companion.
- Follow-up questions: Do repeated disclosures to a companion increase wellbeing, dependency, or both?

### 27. Parks (2023)

Citation and link: Parks, P. (2023). Story circles: A new method of narrative research. American Journal of Qualitative Research, 7(1), 58-72. https://www.ajqr.org/article/story-circles-a-new-method-of-narrative-research-12844

- Research domain: Qualitative research, education.
- Core question: Can story circles generate rich narrative data in group settings?
- Methodology: Method article based on a pre-service teacher identity project.
- Key findings: Story circles can produce large quantities of rich anecdotes by using a focus-group-like setting and shared prompts.
- Evidence quality: Medium.
- Relevance to AI companions: Useful model for creating story-rich environments and prompts.
- Product implications: Build "story circle" modes for families, friends, teams, or user-reflection cohorts with clear norms.
- Limitations: Narrow population and method paper, not broad comparative evidence.
- Follow-up questions: Can an AI facilitate group story circles without dominating or flattening differences?

### 28. Xu et al. (2022)

Citation and link: Xu, Y., Aubele, J., Vigil, V., Bustamante, A. S., Kim, Y.-S., & Warschauer, M. (2022). Dialogue with a conversational agent promotes children's story comprehension via enhancing engagement. Child Development, 93(2), e149-e167. https://doi.org/10.1111/cdev.13708

- Research domain: Child development, HCI, education.
- Core question: Can a conversational agent support story comprehension and engagement?
- Methodology: Empirical study comparing conversational-agent-supported story interaction with adult reading conditions.
- Key findings: Dialogue with a conversational agent can improve story comprehension through engagement.
- Evidence quality: Medium-high.
- Relevance to AI companions: Shows conversational interactivity can improve story engagement, not only deliver content.
- Product implications: Ask story-relevant questions during and after stories; do not treat storytelling as a monologue.
- Limitations: Children, educational setting, and comprehension outcome.
- Follow-up questions: What dialogic reading patterns transfer to adult companion reflection?

### 29. Zhang et al. (2022)

Citation and link: Zhang, Z., Xu, Y., Wang, Y., Yao, B., Ritchie, D., Wu, T., Yu, M., Wang, D., & Li, T. J. J. (2022). StoryBuddy: A human-AI collaborative chatbot for parent-child interactive storytelling with flexible parental involvement. Proceedings of the 2022 CHI Conference on Human Factors in Computing Systems. https://doi.org/10.1145/3491102.3517479

- Research domain: HCI, human-AI collaboration, storytelling.
- Core question: How can AI help parents create interactive storytelling experiences while preserving parental involvement?
- Methodology: Interviews, participatory design, prototype development, and user study.
- Key findings: Parents wanted flexible involvement, educational control, question customization, and lower cognitive load.
- Evidence quality: Emerging.
- Relevance to AI companions: Shows that AI story systems need human agency and configuration, not full automation.
- Product implications: Let users choose story goals, depth, style, and who participates.
- Limitations: Parent-child reading context, not general companionship.
- Follow-up questions: What control knobs make adult users feel ownership over AI-assisted stories?

### 30. Fabre et al. (2025)

Citation and link: Fabre, E., Seaborn, K., Koiwai, S., Watanabe, M., & Riesch, P. (2025). More-than-human storytelling: Designing longitudinal narrative engagements with generative AI. arXiv. https://arxiv.org/abs/2505.23780

- Research domain: HCI, generative AI, longitudinal storytelling.
- Core question: How do people engage over time with a generative AI storytelling agent?
- Methodology: Two-week diary study with 28 participants using a daily AI story app, analyzed with reflexive thematic analysis.
- Key findings: Users valued reflection, creativity, and personalization, but also experienced ambivalence, coherence issues, control issues, and ethical concerns.
- Evidence quality: Emerging.
- Relevance to AI companions: Directly relevant to repeated story interaction with AI.
- Product implications: Longitudinal story agents need memory coherence, user agency, and clear boundaries around emotional bonding.
- Limitations: Preprint, short duration, specific app context.
- Follow-up questions: What memory and control systems prevent long-term AI storytelling from becoming uncanny or frustrating?

## Ten Strongest Findings

1. Transportation is the strongest general construct for whether a story lands.
2. A personal story needs a point. "Evaluation" or "why this matters" is central.
3. Storytelling is co-produced by teller and listener; audience response changes the story.
4. Concrete situation models help listeners understand and remember stories.
5. Emotional movement matters more than static emotional tone.
6. Personal storytelling is identity work and should be treated as sensitive.
7. Self-disclosure creates closeness only when met with responsiveness.
8. Psychological safety increases the likelihood of honest story sharing.
9. Reciprocal turn-taking beats one-sided extraction.
10. AI storytelling systems need user agency, memory coherence, and visible boundaries.

## Implications For AI Companion Design

- Build a story-quality rubric around transportation, tellability, structure, emotional resonance, and responsiveness.
- Ask for stories gradually. Start with low-risk prompts before intimate prompts.
- Treat user anecdotes as living memories whose meaning may change.
- After a user story, respond first as a listener: reflect, validate, clarify, and only then advise if invited.
- Let users control whether a story becomes memory, remains ephemeral, or is deleted.
- Use narrative scaffolds: setting, people, tension, turning point, feeling, lesson, aftermath.
- Add conarration behaviors: callbacks, gentle prompts, remembered details, and "what happened next?"
- Avoid monologue mode unless the user requests it.
- Use fiction, metaphor, and examples for reflection, but label them clearly.
- For group settings, use story-circle norms: prompt, turn order, no interruption, no unsolicited fixing, and time boundaries.

## Prototype Evaluation Metrics

- Transportation: adapted Green & Brock transportation items.
- Narrative engagement: Busselle & Bilandzic dimensions.
- Tellability: user-rated "worth telling/hearing" and "had a point."
- Responsiveness: perceived understanding, validation, and useful follow-up.
- Safety: willingness to share again, perceived control, deletion confidence, and nonjudgment.
- Memory trust: whether remembered story details are accurate, relevant, and welcome.
- Agency: whether the user felt guided, pressured, or interpreted.
