# Storytelling Synthesis By Ideas

Purpose: synthesize the 30-paper storytelling review by recurring ideas, not by individual paper. Use this as the working theory layer for AI companion design.

Companion takeaway: a good storytelling companion should not only generate polished stories. It should create the conditions for stories to emerge, help users shape fragments into meaning, respond well when users disclose something personal, and remember story meaning without becoming intrusive.

## 1. Transportation Is The Core Outcome

Key idea: strong stories absorb attention, create mental imagery, move emotion, and reduce the listener's tendency to counterargue.

Green and Brock (2000) define transportation as a combined state of attention, imagery, and emotion. Their work makes transportation the best top-level measure for whether a story "lands." van Laer et al. (2014) extend this with a meta-analysis showing that both receiver traits and story features shape transportation. Busselle and Bilandzic (2009) break narrative engagement into measurable components: understanding, attentional focus, emotional engagement, and narrative presence.

For AI companions:

- Optimize stories for felt scene entry, not just literary style.
- Track whether the user asks follow-up questions, recalls scenes, or connects the story to their own life.
- Evaluate stories with transportation and narrative-engagement measures, not just thumbs-up ratings.

Design test:

- Compare direct advice against a short story with the same takeaway.
- Measure attention, imagery, emotion, recall, and perceived helpfulness.

Primary citations: Green & Brock (2000); van Laer et al. (2014); Busselle & Bilandzic (2009).

## 2. Good Stories Have A Point

Key idea: a story needs tellability. The listener must understand why this story is worth hearing now.

Labov and Waletzky (1967/1997) show that oral personal narratives often contain orientation, complicating action, evaluation, resolution, and coda. The most important component for companion design is evaluation: the story signals why the event matters. Polanyi (1979) makes the same problem explicit: "so what's the point?" Norrick (2004) adds that tellability can come from humor, shared memory, and listener participation, not only dramatic events.

For AI companions:

- Do not tell stories that are merely decorative.
- Connect a story to the user's current emotional state, question, goal, or memory.
- When helping the user tell a story, ask for the point gently: "What made that moment stick with you?"

Design test:

- Ask users to rate whether a companion story felt relevant, had a point, and was worth hearing.

Primary citations: Labov & Waletzky (1967/1997); Polanyi (1979); Norrick (2004).

## 3. Storytelling Is Performance In Context

Key idea: the same story can succeed or fail depending on timing, audience readiness, setting, and teller-listener relationship.

Bauman (1975) frames verbal art as performance: the teller assumes responsibility for skilled communication before an audience. Sacks (1974) shows how joke and story telling depend on sequential placement, prefaces, audience readiness, and recipiency. Norrick (2004) shows how conversational stories are often conarrated by listeners through laughter, prompts, corrections, and shared reminiscence.

For AI companions:

- A story should be offered, not dumped.
- The companion should detect whether the user wants a story, advice, reflection, or just listening.
- Lightweight permission cues matter: "A quick example?" or "This reminds me of something."

Design test:

- Compare unsolicited stories with permissioned stories.
- Measure interruption, abandonment, perceived relevance, and irritation.

Primary citations: Bauman (1975); Sacks (1974); Norrick (2004).

## 4. People Mostly Tell Small Stories, Not Polished Life Stories

Key idea: everyday storytelling is often partial, messy, fragmentary, and identity-rich.

Georgakopoulou (2006) argues that narrative identity is often performed through "small stories": fragments, updates, projected events, refusals, and quick stance-taking. Ochs and Capps (1996) similarly show that personal narrative is not always complete or coherent; it is socially negotiated and tied to self-understanding. McAdams (2001) describes the broader life-story layer, where people organize identity around agency, communion, redemption, contamination, and coherence.

For AI companions:

- Treat small anecdotes as potentially important, not as failed diary entries.
- Memory should capture both event facts and the user's interpretation.
- The companion should not force every anecdote into a neat moral.

Design test:

- Give users a companion that remembers only facts versus one that remembers facts plus subjective meaning.
- Measure trust, usefulness, and perceived being-known.

Primary citations: Georgakopoulou (2006); Ochs & Capps (1996); McAdams (2001).

## 5. Stories Build Mental Simulations

Key idea: listeners understand stories by constructing situation models: who is involved, where they are, what they want, what changes, and why it matters.

Zwaan and Radvansky (1998) show that comprehension depends on mental models across time, space, causation, protagonist, and goals. Mar (2004) links narrative comprehension and production to social cognition and mentalizing. Oatley (1999) frames fiction as cognitive and emotional simulation: stories let people explore social situations safely.

For AI companions:

- Ground stories in concrete scenes: person, place, time, goal, obstacle, change.
- Use stories to simulate choices before the user acts.
- Make invisible states visible through concrete analogy and scene construction.

Design test:

- Compare abstract coaching to scene-based simulation.
- Measure whether users can explain options, consequences, and feelings more clearly after the interaction.

Primary citations: Zwaan & Radvansky (1998); Mar (2004); Oatley (1999).

## 6. Emotional Movement Matters

Key idea: compelling stories do not merely have emotion; they move through emotion.

Nabi and Green (2015) argue that emotional flow helps sustain engagement and can extend a story's impact. Green and Brock (2000) include emotion as part of transportation. Cohen (2001) shows that identification involves adopting a character's perspective, goals, and emotions. Moyer-Guse (2008) explains how narrative involvement and character involvement can reduce resistance.

For AI companions:

- Avoid one-note emotional tone.
- Build arcs: curiosity, tension, recognition, relief, insight.
- Use emotional movement to support reflection, not to manipulate.

Design test:

- Compare flat supportive responses with responses that contain a clear emotional arc.
- Measure felt understanding, motivation, and perceived pressure.

Primary citations: Nabi & Green (2015); Green & Brock (2000); Cohen (2001); Moyer-Guse (2008).

## 7. Stories Persuade, So They Create Ethical Risk

Key idea: narratives can change beliefs, attitudes, intentions, and behavior. That makes storytelling powerful and ethically sensitive.

Braddock and Dillard (2016) provide meta-analytic evidence that narratives persuade across beliefs, attitudes, intentions, and behaviors. Dahlstrom (2014) shows narratives can improve science communication for nonexpert audiences, but also highlights ethical risk when stories are used persuasively. Moyer-Guse (2008) explains how entertainment narratives may reduce counterarguing and reactance.

For AI companions:

- Do not use stories as covert persuasion.
- Make the source and status of stories clear: true user memory, fictional example, analogy, or sourced anecdote.
- Prefer agency-preserving reflection over behavioral steering.

Design test:

- Ask users whether a story felt supportive, persuasive, manipulative, or clarifying.
- Audit whether stories nudge users toward system goals rather than user goals.

Primary citations: Braddock & Dillard (2016); Dahlstrom (2014); Moyer-Guse (2008).

## 8. Personal Storytelling Is Identity Work

Key idea: when people tell personal stories, they are not only reporting events. They are constructing who they are, what matters, and how they want to be understood.

Ochs and Capps (1996) show that personal narratives organize identity, morality, emotion, and relationships. McAdams (2001) connects life stories to identity themes such as agency, communion, redemption, and coherence. Georgakopoulou (2006) shows that identity is also built in small, everyday narrative fragments.

For AI companions:

- User stories should be handled as sensitive identity material.
- Do not overwrite the user's meaning with the model's interpretation.
- Treat interpretations as revisable: "Last time you framed this as a turning point. Does that still feel true?"

Design test:

- Test memory summaries with user approval.
- Measure whether users feel accurately represented or overinterpreted.

Primary citations: Ochs & Capps (1996); McAdams (2001); Georgakopoulou (2006).

## 9. Disclosure Creates Closeness Only When Met With Responsiveness

Key idea: telling a personal story is not enough. The response determines whether disclosure becomes intimacy, trust, or regret.

Collins and Miller (1994) show through meta-analysis that self-disclosure and liking reinforce each other. Laurenceau et al. (1998) show that intimacy depends not only on disclosure but on perceived partner responsiveness. Aron et al. (1997) show that structured reciprocal disclosure can quickly generate closeness. Sprecher et al. (2013) show that turn-taking disclosure performs better than one-sided disclosure.

For AI companions:

- The first response after a user story should be listening, not advice.
- Good response sequence: reflect content, name emotion carefully, ask consent before interpretation, then offer help.
- Avoid one-sided extraction of intimate stories.

Design test:

- Compare advice-first, reflection-first, and question-first responses after disclosure.
- Measure willingness to share again and perceived understanding.

Primary citations: Collins & Miller (1994); Laurenceau et al. (1998); Aron et al. (1997); Sprecher et al. (2013).

## 10. Psychological Safety Is A Storytelling Environment

Key idea: people tell richer and riskier stories when the environment reduces fear of embarrassment, punishment, misuse, or judgment.

Edmondson (1999) defines psychological safety as a condition for interpersonal risk-taking in teams. Frazier et al. (2017) meta-analyze psychological safety and connect it to learning, information sharing, performance, and voice behavior. Parks (2023) shows how story circles can generate rich narrative material through shared prompts and group structure.

For AI companions:

- Safety is not only tone. It includes privacy, deletion, repair, control, and expectation-setting.
- A storytelling mode should make listening norms explicit through behavior, not long explanation.
- The user should know whether a story is being remembered.

Design test:

- Compare story capture with explicit memory controls versus automatic memory.
- Measure disclosure depth, trust, and regret.

Primary citations: Edmondson (1999); Frazier et al. (2017); Parks (2023).

## 11. Group Storytelling Needs Structure

Key idea: group storytelling improves when the environment manages turn-taking, prompts, attention, and norms.

Parks (2023) presents story circles as a qualitative method for eliciting rich anecdotes in group settings. Sacks (1974) and Norrick (2004) show that oral storytelling depends on audience readiness and listener participation. Bauman (1975) frames the teller as performing for a situated audience, which implies that group format changes the storytelling contract.

For AI companions:

- Group modes should use clear prompts, time boundaries, and turn-taking.
- The AI should facilitate, not become the main performer.
- It should prevent advice-giving or interruption from collapsing a vulnerable story.

Design test:

- Prototype an AI-facilitated story circle for two to four people.
- Measure speaking balance, perceived safety, story richness, and whether users would do it again.

Primary citations: Parks (2023); Sacks (1974); Norrick (2004); Bauman (1975).

## 12. AI Storytelling Should Preserve Human Agency

Key idea: AI storytelling systems work best when they support human control, engagement, and reflection rather than replacing the human storyteller.

Ho, Hancock, and Miner (2018) show that disclosure to chatbots can have psychological, relational, and emotional effects, so even artificial listeners can matter. Xu et al. (2022) show that dialogue with a conversational agent can improve children's story comprehension through engagement. Zhang et al. (2022) show in StoryBuddy that AI storytelling benefits from flexible human involvement and control. Fabre et al. (2025) show that longitudinal generative AI storytelling can produce reflection and creativity, but also raises issues of coherence, control, ambivalence, and ethics.

For AI companions:

- The companion should be a listener, scaffold, editor, and co-reflector before it is a storyteller.
- User control over tone, memory, depth, and participation is central.
- Longitudinal story systems need coherence across sessions and visible boundaries around emotional dependence.

Design test:

- Compare full AI-authored stories with user-guided co-created stories.
- Measure ownership, emotional fit, coherence, and willingness to continue.

Primary citations: Ho et al. (2018); Xu et al. (2022); Zhang et al. (2022); Fabre et al. (2025).

## Cross-Paper Model

The papers converge on this model:

```text
Safety + relevance + concrete scene + emotional movement + responsive listening
= higher transportation, disclosure, memory, and trust
```

For an AI companion, that becomes:

```text
Permissioned prompt
-> user story fragment
-> responsive listening
-> optional scaffold
-> user-approved memory
-> later callback at the right time
```

## Product Principles

1. Ask before entering story mode.
2. Prefer concrete scenes over abstract lessons.
3. Help users find the point; do not impose it.
4. Treat user stories as identity material.
5. Respond before interpreting.
6. Use reciprocal rhythm, not one-sided extraction.
7. Make memory behavior explicit and controllable.
8. Use emotional arcs carefully and ethically.
9. In group contexts, facilitate turn-taking and listening norms.
10. Measure transportation, safety, responsiveness, and agency.
